Executive assessment
Polst has already solved several important problems. The redesign is campaign-aware, the visual system is calm, the screens share a recognizable component language, and the core Polst card is differentiated. The campaign detail screen begins to tell a decision story instead of merely displaying analytics. Distribution has become a system rather than a share modal. The result is credible enough to communicate the product direction.
However, the interface still reads as a polished concept rather than a finished operating environment. The screenshots are consistently styled, but they are not consistently *resolved*. Many screens use the same visual recipe—page title, black action, tabs, white cards, light borders, pale status pills—regardless of the job the page must do. The product therefore looks orderly without always feeling intentional.
Shopify’s polish comes from a deeper stack of decisions:
the shell establishes a strong hierarchy before the page content begins;
each page has a clear job and a dominant reading order;
controls have mature states and predictable behavior;
dense data is organized without making the interface feel cramped;
empty, loading, error, permission, and transitional states are designed as part of the product;
terminology is stable across navigation, tables, filters, and actions;
secondary screens are as resolved as showcase screens;
the visual system disappears behind the task instead of continually announcing “this is a card.”
The current redesign reaches roughly 7.5/10 as a static visual concept and about 5.5/10 as a product-ready system. A prior 9.1/10 audit appears to have over-weighted screenshot similarity, spacing consistency, and component tokenization. Those things matter, but they are only the visible top layer of Shopify’s quality.

What Shopify-level polish actually means
It is not visual imitation
Copying Shopify’s gray canvas, black top bar, white cards, or button styling would produce resemblance, not maturity. Shopify feels polished because its interface expresses a large, stable product model. Navigation, information density, hierarchy, interaction, and error recovery have been refined over years. The useful reference is the discipline behind the pixels.
For Polst, the equivalent discipline should express a different product truth: intent → Polst → vote → decision → memory. Every brand-side screen should help the user define a decision, acquire signal, understand confidence, take the next action, or preserve the result. If a surface cannot explain which part of that chain it serves, it is probably ornamental, duplicated, or at the wrong altitude.
Shopify separates shell, navigation, and content more clearly
Shopify’s black global bar, quiet sidebar, and gray content canvas establish three distinct layers. The eye understands the frame before reading the page. Polst uses similar light values for the header, sidebar, and canvas, then places the entire page inside a large rounded bordered frame. This creates a “dashboard inside a dashboard” effect. The visible internal scrollbar reinforces that impression.
The Polst shell should not copy Shopify’s black header automatically, but it needs equivalent structural contrast. The global header should feel global, navigation should feel persistent, and the page should feel like the working canvas—not another card.
Shopify varies composition according to task
Shopify’s Analytics page is chart-led. Growth is campaign-story-led. Settings is navigation-and-form-led. Search is compact and transient. Polst frequently applies the same composition to every task: title, actions, tabs, cards. The repetition looks systematic, but it reduces product specificity.
The page template should be stable while the content grammar changes. Lists should scan like lists, creation flows should progress like workflows, analytical pages should lead with interpretation, and settings should support safe editing and recovery.
Shopify’s density is purposeful
Shopify can place many elements on screen without feeling dense because secondary information is quiet, repeated structures align precisely, and cards are used where they create meaningful grouping. Polst often increases breathing room by enlarging containers or leaving blank areas. That reduces immediate clutter, but it also lowers information efficiency and creates a mockup-like sense of unused space.
The goal is not “more whitespace.” It is a stronger rhythm between tightly related content and clearly separated sections.
Shopify designs behavior, not just resting appearance
The largest invisible gap is state coverage. A mature admin system must explain what happens while data loads, when it fails, when nothing exists, when permission is missing, when an action is destructive, when a save is in progress, when a background job finishes, and when a user navigates away with unsaved work. None of that can be assessed from a perfect hardcoded state.
Polst will not reach Shopify-level polish through another screenshot-only pass. It must be tested as software.

System-level findings
1. The app is visually consistent but product-semantically repetitive
The same white card, 1 px border, 10–12 px radius, dark heading, and pale pill treatment appears across home summaries, metric cards, forms, readiness lists, distribution counts, settings, billing, and developer gating. This gives the screens family resemblance, but it also flattens meaning. A decision recommendation, a form section, a KPI, and a permission boundary should not all feel like variations of the same container.
Why this matters: Shopify’s components feel consistent because behavior and meaning are consistent, not because everything shares one silhouette.
Fix: define container roles—page section, data card, interactive row, form group, callout, warning, recommendation, preview, and overlay. Each role should have a controlled treatment and interaction contract. Remove cards where spacing or a divider is enough.
2. The shell is too light, too framed, and too internally scrolled
The large content region begins at x≈240, has a border and rounded top corners, and scrolls within the viewport. The global page therefore has two frames: the browser/app shell and the bordered page frame. The top bar, sidebar, and content canvas also sit within a narrow tonal range.
Problems created:
the main content feels like an embedded panel;
the internal scrollbar is visually prominent and competes with the content;
the rounded frame consumes edge space without communicating a meaningful boundary;
long forms and tables feel trapped inside a viewport;
the header search appears detached from the page but not strongly global.
Fix: let the main content canvas own the remaining viewport without a surrounding card border. Use the browser/page scroll for primary scrolling. Keep independent scroll only for true secondary panes or overlays. Establish shell contrast through surface values and a subtle divider, not a thick frame.
3. Typography relies too heavily on boldness
Many headings, row titles, values, navigation labels, buttons, and result labels use high weight. Meanwhile supporting text is small and low contrast. The result is a binary hierarchy—bold black versus small gray—rather than a nuanced type system.
Specific symptoms include 12–13 px metadata competing with 14 px body labels, very bold page titles, condensed metric strips, and multiple bold values on the same line. This makes the interface feel slightly loud despite the muted palette.
Fix: use weight 500 for most labels and row titles, reserve 600–650 for section and page emphasis, and avoid 700 except for exceptional decision statements. Increase critical supporting copy to 14 px/20 px. Build hierarchy with size, spacing, and alignment before weight.
4. The neutral palette lacks both depth and brand intent
Polst uses black text, white cards, a cool light-gray canvas, purple chart/accent elements, and mint status pills. The system is clean, but the surfaces are close enough that boundaries depend heavily on borders. Purple appears as an inherited UI accent rather than a clearly defined part of the decision product.
Fix: create a warmer, more deliberate neutral ladder; reduce the number of visible borders; use purple only for selected state, decision emphasis, and Polst-specific interaction; keep success green distinct from “active” state; use black for primary actions. The OR disc and split-choice geometry should influence small moments in the admin chrome without becoming decorative wallpaper.
5. Border and radius usage is too uniform
Almost every object is rounded and bordered. Inputs, cards, segmented controls, tables, canvas, status panels, callouts, upload areas, and previews all announce their boundaries. This creates visual chatter and makes the design feel constructed from components rather than composed as a page.
Fix: use radii by role: 6 px for small controls, 8 px for inputs/buttons, 10–12 px for true cards, full pill only for compact statuses. Remove borders from layout-only groups. Use a subtle background shift for secondary grouping. Reserve shadow for overlays, menus, and lifted transient surfaces.
6. Action hierarchy is inconsistent
Dark buttons are generally primary, but the verb and placement do not always match the next meaningful step. Examples: “Save draft” dominates unfinished creation flows without a visible “Continue” or “Launch”; campaign detail offers “Review” without clarifying what review means; Distribution has “Assign sources” and “Create source” with ambiguous scope; Polst detail exposes “Generate report” even though campaigns are supposed to own reporting.
Fix: define action tiers and object ownership. Each page gets one dominant next action. Secondary actions remain adjacent but visually quieter. Tertiary actions use text or overflow. Verbs must describe the result: “Continue to distribution,” “Publish campaign,” “Add channel,” “Assign to campaign,” “Review recommendation,” “Export campaign report.”
7. Status language and visual semantics are unstable
Active, Scheduled, Completed, Draft, Assigned, Unassigned, Not started, Leading, Trending, Too close, Ready to decide, and completion percentages appear across pills, plain text, summary rows, and bars. Some are lifecycle states, some are recommendations, some are confidence interpretations, and some are operational readiness. They are visually mixed.
Fix: separate four semantic families:
lifecycle: draft, scheduled, active, paused, completed, archived;
readiness: incomplete, ready, blocked;
decision signal: leading, directional, too close, decisive, inconclusive;
connection state: assigned, unassigned, disconnected, error.
Each family needs its own label rules, icon policy, color tokens, and locations.
8. The information architecture is improved but still partly contradictory
The CEO’s target model is campaign-first, with Polsts as reusable building blocks. The redesign gives campaigns more depth, but global Polsts and global Analytics remain equally prominent in the sidebar. Individual Polst detail still includes report and deep audience information, which risks recreating the original inversion.
Fix: make Home and Campaigns the primary work surfaces. Keep Polsts as a library. Global Analytics should be workspace trends and cross-campaign learning only; it should never compete with campaign decision analysis. Individual Polst detail should focus on content, reusable performance history, and where the Polst is used—not campaign-owned attribution or reporting.
9. Storytelling exists in one screen, not as a product rule
Campaign detail’s “Recommended: Option B +18 pts” is the closest screen to the product promise. It states a conclusion, explains why, identifies a watch item, and gives a next action. This should be the governing pattern everywhere decisions are summarized.
Home currently greets the user and lists objects. Analytics shows metrics. Distribution counts sources. None consistently answer: What changed? Why does it matter? What needs attention? What should I do next?
Fix: create a reusable Decision Narrative pattern:
status headline;
confidence or evidence level;
what changed;
likely cause;
caveat;
recommended next action;
link to supporting evidence.
10. Real states are absent, so polish cannot be trusted
The interface has no demonstrated loading skeletons, empty workspaces, failed requests, permission constraints, stale data warnings, optimistic updates, undo, background exports, upload progress, duplicate names, partial saves, or offline behavior. Form controls do not show validation or error recovery. Tables do not show zero results. Charts do not show insufficient data.
Fix: make state design a deliverable, not an implementation afterthought. Every critical component and flow needs a state matrix before visual sign-off.
11. The data layer looks plausible but not authentic
The North Star Pantry workspace uses curated generic food photography and invented metrics. The data is internally plausible, but the repeated editorial imagery reads as a demo library rather than one real company’s product catalogue. This weakens trust precisely where Polst is selling defensible decision intelligence.
Fix: build one coherent demo account with a real-seeming catalogue, campaign chronology, branded product assets, consistent dates, realistic channel names, believable traffic volumes, and traceable calculations. Every number on Home, Analytics, Campaign, and Polst detail should reconcile.
12. Secondary depths are visibly unfinished
Campaign detail is deep. Audience, Analytics Insights/Reports, channel creation, developer platform, billing, and parts of settings are thin or placeholders. “Mock billing state” should never appear in a leadership or investor demo. A product feels as polished as the second unexpected click.
Fix: either complete a route or hide it behind an honest “coming later” boundary. Do not expose rooms with placeholder furniture.
13. Accessibility is suggested, not proven
The interface appears to include accessible labels and contrast-conscious text, but several patterns are risky: icon-only controls, color-coded status pills, low-contrast tertiary text, segmented controls, visual progress bars, drag/reorder assumptions, image choice cards, and custom chart interactions.
Fix: run a keyboard-only path through create → schedule → distribute → review. Verify focus order, focus-visible appearance, escape behavior in overlays, screen-reader labels, chart summaries, status text independent of color, 200% zoom, and reduced motion.
14. Responsive behavior is unresolved
The left navigation, centered global search, two-column details, three-column Polst grid, wide data tables, internal scroll container, and side-by-side image composer all depend on a wide desktop viewport.
Fix: design at 1280, 1024, 768, and 390 px. At tablet widths, collapse the sidebar and convert secondary panels into drawers or stacked sections. On mobile, use one primary action, sticky save/continue where necessary, card-based table fallbacks, and vertically stacked binary options while preserving the OR relationship.
Screen-by-screen audit
Home
Figure 2. Current Polst Home.
What works: the page is calmer than an analytics dashboard; it introduces active campaigns and Polsts; “What’s next” points toward action; the page supports the CEO’s desire for a story and planning layer.
What is wrong:
The centered “Good afternoon, Max.” is too prominent for an operational product and consumes the strongest position without adding decision value.
The period selector and “View full analytics” imply that the top strip is analytics-led, partially recreating the wrong-altitude problem.
Four metrics are compressed into one horizontal card, making the card read like a dashboard widget rather than a narrative status.
Sparkline fragments are decorative because they have no baseline, time context, tooltips, or comparison explanation.
Campaigns and Polsts receive near-equal visual weight even though campaigns should be primary.
The Active/Queued segmented controls repeat inside both panels, increasing control noise.
“What’s next” begins below the fold; the most action-oriented section is visually subordinate.
The inner viewport clips the lower cards and makes the page feel trapped.
Redesign direction: replace the greeting hero with a compact workspace header and a single decision briefing: “Two campaigns need attention; Packaging Direction Test is ready to decide.” Place the next actions directly beneath. Keep workspace health secondary and move full trend analytics to Analytics. Campaigns get the dominant wide column; recent/reusable Polsts become a compact supporting list.
Polsts library
Figure 3. Current visual Polsts library.
What works: the grid foregrounds the product’s unique visual object; the shared card supports parity with the consumer experience; statuses and calls to action are visible.
What is wrong:
The three-column grid is visually rich but inefficient for administration. It prioritizes imagery over scanability, ownership, reuse, status, campaign association, and last activity.
Title wrapping and option truncation vary, creating uneven hierarchy.
Result pills, OR discs, image crops, status pills, option headers, response counts, and footer actions compete within a small card.
The first card’s selected option is purple while others are gray, but the meaning—winner, user selection, or live voter state—is not explicit in an admin library.
Active, Scheduled, Draft, and Completed cards share almost the same frame; lifecycle differentiation relies on tiny pills.
Grid/list toggle exists, but the grid appears to be the default without evidence that brand operators prefer it.
Images dominate even when a Polst is a reusable building block rather than the primary entity.
Bottom-row cards are clipped by the internal scrolling frame.
Redesign direction: default to a dense list for campaign operators, with thumbnail pair, title, status, campaign usage, response count, decision signal, updated time, and overflow actions. Retain the grid as a deliberate “visual library” mode. Separate a neutral thumbnail preview from a live interactive Polst card to avoid implying that the library card itself is a voter surface.

Campaign list

What works: campaigns are treated as first-class objects; the table is much more scannable than the Polst grid; responses, Polst count, event, readiness, and contextual actions are useful.
What is wrong:
“Readiness” mixes decision interpretation (Leading, Too close, Trending), lifecycle (Draft, Not started), and operational state.
Row actions change labels across the table but do not share a stable action hierarchy.
The table has no sorting indicators, column customization, bulk selection, saved views, owner, updated time, or clear pagination/virtualization behavior.
Filters use a segmented control for six states; this will become cramped and does not support combinations.
Search and filter controls occupy separate sides without a unified toolbar model.

Campaign detail
Figure 7. Current campaign overview.
What works: this is the strongest screen. The recommendation leads, the rationale includes a caveat, the completion rate is contextualized, and “Next action” supports the CEO’s story requirement. Tabs give the campaign room to own Polsts, Distribution, Insights, Report, and Settings.
What is wrong:
“Recommended: Option B +18 pts” is useful but lacks confidence, sample sufficiency, and explicit decision status.
“Review” is vague. Review what—the recommendation, the campaign, or collected responses?
“Share / Embed” conflicts with the Distribution tab and global channel system.
Campaign health is another passive key-value card; it could be compact metadata.
Voter journey uses thick progress bars but does not explain whether drops are expected, significant, or source-specific.
“–18% biggest drop” is red but not connected to a recommended action or diagnostic drill-down.
The Next action card repeats a paragraph and a small button rather than integrating the action with the recommendation.
Source snapshot begins below the fold, delaying the “why” behind the recommendation.
There is no visible freshness timestamp, comparison period, confidence methodology, anomaly explanation, or decision log.
Redesign direction: make a Decision Brief the page’s dominant object: recommendation, evidence strength, caveats, last refresh, and action. Place source explanation and journey diagnostics directly beneath. Convert static health metadata into a compact header band. Use tabs for deeper analysis but keep the overview self-sufficient.

What works: the page is readable; the response trend has an honest axis; source, device, and platform mixes are immediately understandable; “Ready to decide” points toward product value.
What is wrong:
The page is still metric-led rather than decision-led.
“Ready to decide: 2” is the most product-specific KPI but is not clickable or explained.
“Top source: Website” lacks context—is it highest volume, highest completion, best quality, or strongest lift?

Source mix, Devices, and Platforms repeat the same horizontal-bar grammar, creating visual monotony.

Distribution
Figure 10. Polst Distribution and Shopify Growth. Shopify organizes channels around marketing performance and action; Polst currently counts assets and lists rows.
What works: distribution is elevated into an organization-level system; source performance includes responses and completion; different channel types can coexist.
What is wrong:
“Channels,” “Sources,” “Links &amp; embeds,” “QR codes,” and “Influencer links” overlap conceptually.
The top four cards count inventory rather than answering whether distribution is healthy.
“Assign sources” and “Create source” do not use marketer-native language.
The tabs repeat the top-card categories, producing duplicated navigation.
Source Performance mixes channel definition, linked object, performance, assignment, and activity in one table without a clear primary task.
“Assigned” is not meaningful without saying assigned to what or how many campaigns.
Instagram Story Link is unassigned yet has 214 responses, which may be valid historically but needs explanation.
There is no visible create-channel flow, credential status, QR preview, link copy action, embed health, expired token warning, or attribution quality.
Multiple QR codes are a core CEO requirement, but the screen does not show naming conventions, physical placement, campaign assignment, or lifecycle.
Redesign direction: define Channel as the reusable organization-level connection (Website, Email, Instagram, QR, Influencer) and Source as a campaign-specific tracked instance (Conference Booth QR, Newsletter link). Let users create/manage channels, then create or assign tracked sources inside campaigns. The global page should show channel health, reusable assets, attribution quality, and issues—not just totals.
Settings and team
Figure 11. Polst Settings and Shopify Settings. Shopify uses dedicated navigation, clearer edit affordances, stronger grouping, and more explicit ownership.
What works: settings are grouped into brand profile, workspace preferences, and embed appearance; the live embed preview is relevant; team roles and invitations are visible.
What is wrong:
Settings remain inside the normal product shell even though the information architecture is large enough to justify a dedicated settings navigation model.
Many fields look read-only because the visual affordance for editable controls is weak.
Save behavior is fragmented: “Save changes” in one card and “Save” in another, with no autosave or dirty-state model.
The two top cards have different content heights, leaving awkward internal whitespace.
Appearance, default date range, report format, and visibility are unrelated preferences grouped by convenience.
Embed appearance is a large configuration surface but appears below general settings, which weakens ownership.
Typeface and corner-radius customization may create unsupported combinations; token-level preview and validation are absent.
Team appears as content lower on the same long settings page, not as a distinct settings destination.
“Developer platform — Pro” is an unfinished gate with no upgrade or learn-more behavior.
“Mock billing state” openly breaks product credibility and should be removed from all demos.
Role tags such as Founder, Marketing Director, and Analyst are displayed beside authorization roles without clarifying whether they are job titles or permissions.
Redesign direction: adopt a dedicated settings surface with local navigation: Workspace, Brand, Embed, Team &amp; permissions, Billing, Developer. Use explicit view/edit modes or autosave per section. Keep permission roles separate from optional job titles. Hide unavailable modules or present a complete upgrade boundary.
Global search
Figure 12. Polst Search and Shopify Search. Polst provides useful results but the overlay is visually overbuilt and lacks a demonstrated keyboard model.
What works: search spans campaigns, Polsts, and sources; filters are available; results include a title and context; the global shortcut is visible in the shell.
What is wrong:
The modal is tall and narrow relative to its content; it feels like a dialog rather than a fast command/search layer.
The large circular close control with purple outline is more visually dominant than search itself.
The overlay blur softens the entire product and feels decorative compared with Shopify’s simpler dimming layer.
Result type labels sit far to the right with weak column alignment.
There is no visible selected result, hover state, keyboard navigation, recent queries, empty state, loading state, or “see all results.”
Filter chips consume vertical space and use another pill pattern.
The result icon is generic and does not help distinguish Campaign, Polst, and Source quickly.
Search scope and ranking are unclear; “All” may mix objects in ways that become noisy at scale.
Redesign direction: use a wider, shorter command palette. Auto-focus the input. Add arrow-key selection, Enter to open, Escape to close, recent items, object-specific icons/thumbnails, highlighted query matches, and a final “View all results” row. Use an icon-only quiet close affordance or rely on Escape/outside click.
Recommended design direction
Visual thesis: Calm decision intelligence
Polst should feel less like a generic SaaS dashboard and more like a trusted decision workbench. The interface should be quiet enough for serious analysis, fast enough for marketers, and distinct at moments where a binary choice becomes a recommendation.
The admin chrome should remain neutral and compact. Polst identity should appear through split-choice geometry, the OR disc, decisive black-and-white contrast, and restrained purple used for selection and evidence—not through more decorative branding.

Use a 4 px spacing base. Recommended spacing steps: 4, 8, 12, 16, 20, 24, 32, 40, 48, 64. Avoid one-off 14/18/22/26 px gaps unless optical correction is documented.
Use 6 px radius for compact controls, 8 px for inputs and buttons, 10–12 px for true cards, and full pills only for status/filters that genuinely behave as pills. Use no shadow on standard cards; use a restrained shadow only for menus, popovers, modals, and draggable surfaces.
Typography direction
Use one highly legible UI family with compact metrics and a slightly distinctive voice. Geist Sans is a practical open-source option; a licensed grotesk such as Söhne or ABC Diatype would create more brand character. Do not change type merely to avoid a generic look; validate numerals, tables, long campaign names, and dense metadata.

Component rules
Buttons: 36 px default height, 32 px compact, 40 px only for high-emphasis onboarding or marketing contexts. One primary action per region. Icon-only buttons require tooltip and accessible name. Destructive actions cannot share the primary black treatment.
Inputs: 36–40 px control height; persistent label above; helper or error text below; visible hover/focus/disabled states; validation cannot rely on color alone. Editable fields must not look like passive key-value rows.
Tabs: use for stable peer views within one object. Do not use tabs and segmented controls interchangeably. Avoid more than five or six top-level tabs without overflow or grouping.
Statuses: use a dot only when it adds meaning. Lifecycle statuses use neutral/blue/green tokens; decision signal uses a separate evidence vocabulary; warnings use amber; errors use red. Do not label “Active” in bright success green if active is merely normal operation.
Tables: define sorting, row navigation, hover, keyboard focus, empty state, loading state, pagination, bulk selection, column behavior, and overflow. Right-align quantitative columns. Keep one stable primary row destination.
Charts: rebuild on SVG/canvas with scale definitions, tooltips, keyboard focus, accessible summaries, comparison periods, selection, export, and zero/insufficient-data states. Every chart needs a one-sentence “what this means” or a linked insight.
Polst preview: distinguish content preview, voter interaction, and result state. The admin should never confuse a static thumbnail with a live voteable surface. Preserve exact parity where “what your audience sees” is promised.

Priority plan
P0 — Make the product truthful
Wire one complete golden path to real or coherent fixture data: create campaign → add Polst → assign channels → collect responses → receive recommendation → export report. Add loading, empty, error, save, validation, and permission states. Replace generic imagery with a coherent North Star Pantry asset set. Reconcile every displayed number.
Exit condition: a user can complete the path, refresh at any point without losing work, and understand any failure without developer assistance.
P1 — Correct the shell and hierarchy
Remove the enclosing content frame and nested primary scrollbar. Establish global/header/sidebar/canvas contrast. Standardize page headers and action hierarchy. Make Home and Campaigns primary; clarify the role of Polsts and workspace Analytics.
Exit condition: users can identify where they are, what the page is for, and the next action within five seconds.
P2 — Rebuild the critical workflows
Turn campaign and Polst creation into staged flows with autosave and review. Clarify Channel versus Source. Remove campaign-owned analytics/reporting from individual Polst detail. Complete settings navigation and team/permission handling.
Exit condition: the object model is reflected consistently in navigation, labels, actions, URLs, and data ownership.
P3 — Make decision intelligence credible
Create the Decision Brief pattern, evidence-strength vocabulary, confidence/caveat system, and action recommendations. Rebuild chart infrastructure and connect anomalies to explanations and next actions.
Exit condition: every analytical surface can answer “what happened, why it matters, and what to do next.”
P4 — Finish the product map
Complete or hide Audience, Insights, Reports, channel creation, Billing, and Developer surfaces. Remove placeholders from demo routes. Add responsive models and verified accessibility.
Exit condition: two levels of exploratory navigation never reveal mock states, dead actions, or unstyled placeholders.

Launch-quality acceptance criteria
The redesign should not be called Shopify-level until all of the following are true:
one complete user journey is backed by persistent data and survives refresh;
every primary page has loading, empty, error, permission, and zero-result states;
campaign, Polst, channel, and source ownership is consistent in UI and data model;
every page has one clear dominant action;
no visible placeholder, mock billing text, dead control, or unexplained gated room remains;
the shell uses natural page scrolling and no decorative enclosing frame;
quantitative values reconcile across Home, Campaign, Polst, Distribution, and Analytics;
status labels belong to documented semantic families;
charts support tooltips, accessible summaries, comparison context, and insufficient-data states;
forms support autosave or explicit save status, validation, unsaved changes, and recovery;
keyboard-only completion of the golden path is possible;
200% zoom, reduced motion, and screen-reader landmarks have been verified;
layouts are resolved at 1280, 1024, 768, and 390 px;
a visual regression suite covers shell, lists, detail, creation, search, overlays, tables, and key states;
product language is reviewed for marketer comprehension and uses stable nouns/verbs;
a reviewer can click two levels beyond the demo path without finding an unfinished surface.

Final verdict
The current redesign is a strong direction, not a finished benchmark match. It has solved much of the staging interface’s visible disorder and moved toward the CEO’s campaign-first model. Its best screen—the campaign overview—proves that Polst can feel like decision software rather than a polling dashboard.
The next leap will not come from another pass on radius, shadows, or spacing in isolation. It will come from making the interface truthful, completing the state and interaction systems, clarifying object ownership, turning analytics into decisions, and enforcing the same quality at every depth.
Shopify’s lesson is not “use more cards.” It is: make every product decision feel inevitable, every state feel anticipated, and every action feel safe. That is the standard Polst should now design toward.
Evidence and limitations
This audit is based on the supplied static screenshots and three internal product notes. It does not inspect the running implementation, DOM, design tokens, API behavior, performance, actual keyboard flow, screen-reader output, responsive layouts, or production data. Interaction and state findings are therefore framed as missing evidence or required validation, not as claims that no implementation exists anywhere.
